üéØ The Challenge

Nigeria, like much of Africa, has endured severe consequences in its ongoing battle against terrorism. Between 2011 and 2018, Boko Haram insurgents were directly responsible for the deaths of more than 27,000 civilians and military personnel (Vanda Felbab-Brown, 2018). In Borno State alone, over 1,320 IED-related incidents were recorded between 2011 and 2024, resulting in approximately 4,800 fatalities (ACLED; Truth Nigeria). These statistics highlight a critical need for innovative technologies that can detect and neutralize explosive threats before they claim more lives.

üõ†Ô∏è What I Built

In response to this challenge, I developed a prototype autonomous robot designed to detect Improvised Explosive Devices (IEDs). This project served as the culmination of my final-year studies and represented a true interdisciplinary engineering challenge combining artificial intelligence, control systems, electronics, and mechanical CAD design. My goal was to create a compact, efficient, and intelligent system capable of identifying potential threats and contributing to safer environments.

üí∏ Funding Constraints

IED detection systems typically require extensive resources and field testing. Operating with a budget of roughly $100, I had to make every decision count. This limitation became an opportunity to innovate prioritizing essential functions, sourcing affordable components, and optimizing performance using readily available hardware. These constraints sharpened my creativity and deepened my understanding of embedded systems and system integration.

System Design

The ESP32 microcontroller serves as the central processing unit, leveraging its dual-core architecture and Wi-Fi nnectivity, and over 30 GPIO pins to orchestrate all system operations. It wirelessly communicates with the host PC and issues precise control signals to actuators. The ESP32-CAM module captures and streams live video frames to enable real-time computer vision processing using YOLOv11. Orientation tracking is handled by the MPU6050 IMU, which provides angular position data via I2C for PID-based heading correction. Obstacle detection relies on an HC-SR04 ultrasonic sensor mounted on an SG90 servo motor, enabling 180¬∞ environmental scanning. Motor control is managed through the L298N driver, which regulates speed and direction of six DC motors across the rocker-bogie chassis. An IRLZ44N MOSFET switches the high-current buzzer for threat alerts. Power is supplied by a 4S2P 18650 lithium battery pack, stepped down to a stable 5V rail via an LM2596 buck converter, with a 4S BMS ensuring protection against overcharge and deep discharge. The entire structure is built from 25mm PVC pipes and elbows, forming a lightweight, durable, and terrain-adaptive frame.

‚öôÔ∏è Engineering Insights

Among the many technical challenges I encountered, one stood out: interference between the ultrasonic sensors and the servo motor. I noticed erratic movements in both systems, which compromised stability and sensor accuracy. Initially, I relied on existing software libraries to manage the hardware, but the interference persisted. This prompted me to abandon high-level abstractions and work directly with the microcontroller‚Äôs hardware registers.

Through further investigation, I discovered the underlying issue: servo motors require a continuous stream of precise PWM signals (typically every 20 ms) to maintain position, while ultrasonic sensors operate by transmitting high-frequency sound pulses and timing their echoes. These overlapping timing operations within the main program loop were causing signal conflicts leading to jittery servo movements and unreliable distance readings.

‚è±Ô∏è The Breakthrough: Timer Interrupts

The breakthrough came when I utilized timer interrupts to decouple the timing-critical operations. By configuring a dedicated timer interrupt to refresh the servo position every 20 ms through an Interrupt Service Routine (ISR), I ensured that servo control signals remained stable and consistent, independent of the main program loop. This allowed the ultrasonic sensors to operate asynchronously without interference‚Äîcompletely eliminating jitter and restoring accurate distance measurements.

üìö Key Takeaway

This experience reinforced a vital engineering lesson: understanding hardware-level operations is essential when working with embedded systems. Abstraction layers can simplify development, but true optimization often requires diving deeper into the fundamentals where precision and control truly matter.
